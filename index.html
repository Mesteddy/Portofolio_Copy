NCRP Mission

Crop diseases can have a significant impact on agricultural productivity. According to the Crop Disease and Agricultural Productivity report (see section 1.4), invasive plant pathogens, including fungi, cause an estimated $21 billion in crop losses yearly in the United States. Gardens of all sizes though, like community gardens, greenhouses, and indoor gardens are affected similarly. These gardens can be an important source of food and well-being for lots of people and communities, but the resources required to maintain those gardens can often be cost-prohibitive and hard to come by. Indoor gardening has seen an incredible spike in popularity since the onset of the pandemic in 2020, and we aim to offer a unique solution to new gardeners with our system, mitigating plant loss and monitoring the progress of plants throughout their life cycle. While solutions do exist on the market for monitoring crop and plant health, our system is built to be a low-cost, easy-to-use, and powerful tool that targets customers with modest garden sizes. Our system is unique in that it is even deployable in environments like apartments, as the drone operates at such a low noise level. Our Machine Learning Algorithm will detect life events in a plant and can alert the user about these changes. We aim to save many plant lives that would otherwise be lost to avoidable diseases. We trained our algorithm on images of healthy and diseased plants so that it can learn how to tell the differences between them and make judgments for itself. As we take more pictures, we used positive ID’s to retrain the model so that it can continue to learn. .

In our application, the autonomous control of the DJI Tello drone is facilitated through our Python-based GUI built with Tkinter and ThemedTk. The drone autonomously scans the user's garden without requiring direct control. This scanning process can be initiated through the GUI where users manage their gardens and observe real-time scanning progress. The combination of our Python GUI, drone autonomy, and AWS-backed data storage and processing strategy enables a seamless and personalized garden monitoring experience. Users can track their garden's health dynamically, with all data gathered by the autonomous drone efficiently stored, processed, and presented for their unique needs. While the client's local machine performs temporary/short-term data storage, any extended storage is held in S3 and DynamoDB. This is vital as the drone lacks local storage and our system requires access to previously captured images and data. Moreover, each data point is user-specific, correlating to the individual's unique garden layout and plant health status. Throughout the garden scanning process by the drone, data is initially sent to local storage before being moved to AWS for processing. This data, including images, is user-specific, ensuring that the machine learning platform and the React application receive relevant and personalized data through API requests. Our API, built using Node Js, not only queries and supplies data from DynamoDB but also provides the React application with data to visualize each plant's health status, the garden's weekly report chart, and individual plant line charts, all user-specific. The AWS SES feature is utilized to send notifications about significant, user-specific data collected, including alerts of any disease detections in the user's garden from our ML model. The React application further allows users to customize their experience. Users can reupload pictures of the plant directly, edit each plant name and if they choose to, change the disease label which was detected for a given plant. A dropdown on each plant modal view enables users to select their plant's genus. Once the genus is chosen, the table under the selected plant image populates with pertinent, user-specific data. This data includes details such as life cycle, sun requirements, soil pH preferences, minimal cold hardiness, flowering time, suitable locations, container preferences, etc., all tailored to the specific plant genus selected by the user.
